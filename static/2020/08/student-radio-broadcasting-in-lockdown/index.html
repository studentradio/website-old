Student Radio: Broadcasting in Lockdown

 19th August 2020 |  Members News, Station Focus

This article was written by Jelena Sofronijevic, George Richmond, Ciara Treacy, Uma Suri, Matt Webb, Ellie Bladon and Oliver Morgan. 

The Covid-19 lockdown has changed the way we communicate with our friends and family, instead of going to see each other in person we’ve all moved to video calls for many, many quizzes and casual catch-ups. This has been no different for broadcasting, with many students not able to physically get into their studios due to the restrictions. 

That said, a lot of the student stations from up and down the country haven’t let that stop them. They’ve been coming up with different ideas and methods for keeping their shows on air. 

We spoke to a few stations to get their views on how the lockdown has changed the way that they think about their shows and going about their content. 

 

Jelena Sofronijevic – Fresh Air 

@jelsofron 

For three years and three Edinburgh Festivals, I have produced and presented political-cultural programmes for http://FreshAir.org.uk, the University of Edinburgh’s student radio station. As Head of Arts (2019-2020), I hosted The Arts Show, a weekly live broadcast featuring exclusive interviews, reviews, and features from Edinburgh’s diverse arts scene – from theatre to literature, film to the visual arts. Without a studio, guests, or live streaming platform, the lockdown has forced me to reconsider what and how I produce.

Yet, responding to these challenges has expanded my content and skillset. Swapping live radio for podcasts gave me the opportunity to more deeply research ideas. Featuring on The Arts Show in Lockdown, I use individual artists or works as starting points to interrogate wider questions including gender, imperialism, and commercialisation in the arts. I have also developed a podcast documentary series around sustainability, including INDIAscussion, and an upcoming project with Tortoise Media. Zoom and CleanFeed have enabled me to interview guests the world over, whether Italian environmental scientists or farmers from New Zealand. (My advice – a heavy blanket shroud is a fantastic soundproofing and conversation starter). Through trial and error – and video tutorials – I have improved my editing skills. I am still grappling with constructing a story and sense of place without clips recorded on location, and would appreciate any recommendations…  

The Arts Show and The Arts Show in Lockdown

George Richmond – CamFM

 @ChatwGeorge 

Using the internet in normal times can be hard in the countryside but being exiled there from uni because of a global pandemic and still wanting to broadcast led to new challenges. These included the need to walk to the nearby farm to upload the show online, due to poor home wifi. 

Some things did get easier. For my weekly interview-based show, ‘Chat With George’, the interviews became much easier. No longer was the extra-organising attached to the physical presence of interviewees, required. Instead, I came to tolerate the worlds of ‘Zoom’ and ‘Skype’, like so many across the country. Even whilst the interviews often depended on the guests’ ability to use these technologies, it did allow me to interview Roger Mosey, the former BBC executive and the MP, Richard Graham.

 

Crucially, this pandemic has shown many including myself, especially with the TIMES Radio emerging amidst it, the importance of radio being there for people even in the face of a depressing news cycle. I realise now that though listener numbers may not always seem to make radio worth it, the passion and support of the Cam FM team and the two-way support between myself and my loyal listeners, certainly does. 


 

 

Ciara Treacy – Forge Radio 

At the beginning of lockdown, Forge Radio set up the ‘Isolation Pod’, a podcast where we chose different themes every week, from ‘how we were coping during lockdown’, to ‘the games we played during quarantine’. We called on the University of Sheffield’s students to participate and share their quarantine tales, pulling everyone together and reminding ourselves we are not alone during this sometimes isolating period. The best part about the Isolation Pod was that anyone could take part, all you needed was a phone – which was quite refreshing to see how easy it can be to make brilliant content. 

We were met with challenges, firstly trying to communicate as effectively as we can as a new radio committee without working together in person before. Whilst it being the summer holidays, it was important to find a balance between giving people space, and hosting the occasional meeting to make sure that everyone is happy with the direction the station is going in and feeling comfortable together virtually. Another challenge is keeping the radio station on the air. Without being able to physically enter the studio due to COVID, if the studio goes offline, (which it did!), it was vital to communicate with the relevant people effectively, and without concerning the rest of the committee. Knowing who to message was important, and a healthy relationship with our SU made it possible to fix our tech issues with ease. 

The radio industry’s consistency alone has brought a veil of normality for listeners. Having the music we know and love being played day in, day out has made staying in the house so much easier. Listening to how other people are coping during this unforeseen period is a reassurance. From Forge’s podcasts, it is so nice to know that we are in this together, knowing it will not be long till we are back together again.

 


 

 

Uma Suri – CamFM

Surviving lockdown with radio

I worried that during lockdown my life would contract into increasingly tense interactions with the same four people and an unresponsive cat. Thankfully, radio helped mitigate this, not least by giving me something to do. Presenting a show is also a great chance to just talk and share music- to an unknown and potentially unlimited audience.

Practically, I’ve had to pre-record shows for the first time, and therefore teach myself how to use Audacity while crouched in the gap under my desk- a poor imitation of our soundproofed studio! A big challenge was striking a balance between embracing the chance to edit and perfect parts and re-recording every little piece because I had made a minor mistake. In the end, I avoided re-doing segments because I wanted to maintain the freshness and energy I feel when broadcasting live. It’s also exciting to have to innovate a bit- one highlight was having a friend co-present the show via Zoom. 

I know a lot of students who started listening to their uni radio stations in lockdown. My own experience, plus the impressive turnout of Cam FM presenters in difficult times, revealed how helpful making radio was in maintaining connections- to the university, to friends and the outside world more generally. 

Uma Suri

The Punctured Bicycle Show, on Cam FM

 

 

 

Matt Webb – URN 

Adapting Shows, many co-hosts aren’t going to be in the same household, so the natural interaction and banter that would normally flow in a studio is harder to maintain over a call. Trying to keep that same nature of bouncing off one another has been harder to do, and one effective way to combat this has been having a simultaneous video call. 

Challenges/Overcoming – Moving swiftly from operating in a studio to broadcasting remotely was extremely difficult to do, but thankfully we managed to never go off the air, utilising remote access to the technology alongside clean feed to overcome this challenge. 

How has the radio industry helped? – The radio industry has been crucial in keeping people grounded to a sense of normality during the lockdown, with the national figures reflecting this student wide trend. On a student level, it’s been a great way to remind students of the sense of community that exists at University.

 

 

 

Ellie Bladon – CamFM 

@eleanor_bladon

I started lockdown a little earlier than most by catching COVID myself. I missed the preparations to isolate that others had and felt like I’d suddenly been thrown from normal life into a very small world. I went from working full-time in a lab for my PhD to spending all my time in bed coughing. As I slowly recovered in the weeks that followed, I struggled to do the simplest things without feeling like I’d run a marathon, and this left very little motivation for work. The one thing I found I could get excited about was creating radio.

 

Previously I would broadcast my science interview show, Us and STEMM, live from Cam FM’s fully-equipped studio with my guest opposite me, but in lockdown, I needed to innovate. The bunk beds in our spare bedroom became my studio. It turns out that if you drape blankets over the lower bunk and hide in the dark you get pretty good acoustics. And without the expectation that my guests would be physically present, I gained potential interviewees from around the world. My furthest Zoom interview during lockdown was to an amazing scientist in Nigeria!

I loved the motivation that radio has given me so much that I started a new show with my isolating partner, aka my husband, Andrew. Understandably, most of the news was focusing on COVID, meaning that awesome non-COVID science wasn’t getting the air-time it deserved. Our new show, Lockdown Science, is a light-hearted look at the best non-COVID science news of the fortnight.

I’ve always loved being part of Cam FM, but the lockdown has shown me just how big a part of my life it is, and how valuable a creative outlet can be when other things in life aren’t quite going to plan.

Ellie Bladon, Head of Publicity and presenter of “Us and STEMM” and “Lockdown Science”

 

 

 

Oliver Morgan – Forge Radio

@OliverMorgs 

My show has changed dramatically compared to what my usual broadcasts would be like, but that doesn’t necessarily mean that the format alterations are a bad thing – if anything, it’s part of the experience of making a student radio show! It feels like the rule book has been completely ripped up, but it’s meant that the show has evolved to become what it is today – it has certainly been more fun, making a show DIY-style! 

Not only has the music element been taken away, but it was at first difficult to imagine what it would be like to have guests or a co-host on the show too, so I started off creating podcast-style shows which only focussed on me. I wanted to share just a pinch of what the old show before COVID-19 was like, and so what I did was do something that my live radio show doesn’t necessarily have – and this was to create a plan! 

When speaking and recording shows with other people in general conversation, it’s so easy to overrun and go on crazy tangents, but the aim of the game with accommodating the fact that we’re not in the studio is to set a time limit – and by hook or by crook stick to it! What this all means is that it’s been the dreaded ‘J’ word – it has been a journey, but one which has been fun to experience and one in which I have learnt a great deal about how I work, and how I can adapt relatively swiftly to continue creating content.

Life getting in the way was a great challenge! As I’ve been trying to catch-up with work over the holidays as well as doing revision and attending remote lectures, my timetable has been turned upside-down! So, when I have been trying to organise my life alongside the timetables of other people, the most difficult part was suggesting a time to record another show – and then committing to it! Of course, hosting a student radio show under normal circumstances is a commitment that you stick to (most of the time), but when life is both full of times of sheer ‘nothingness’ and times when the day seems to get filled with a list of jobs as long as your arm, it has just been quite a challenge to accommodate virtual meetings with others. 

It always makes you feel guilty when you say that you have to cancel or change times, but the up-side of having this as a challenge is that the thing which most people blame (that being the internet) was the thing which turned out to be the most reliable! It was at first a struggle to find a piece of software to record audio at an acceptable quality, but when I found it, the production side of the radio show has been a veritable breeze compared to working out the nuts and bolts of the new show! It has meant that I have been able to continue creating content into the summer break and has allowed me to experiment with altering the show’s usual structure. The challenges that I have faced have been more of a help than a hindrance – it has let me break free to try and find new ways of doing things, many of which I will take with me going forward.  

My Twitter timeline has been constantly reminding me that even though we have been apart, we have always been together, strangely. However, there is only so much value in reading text on a screen – and what the radio industry has done extremely well (judging this from a listener’s perspective) is create a greater depth of human connection – more than a generic Tweet or Facebook post could ever do. 

It’s the sense of community and the fact that radio isn’t an echo chamber for negativity which has made radio secure itself as the bedrock of socialising and connecting with others over the past few months. To this end, it feels as if the whole nation has re-connected with radio as they have tried to entertain themselves or inform their decisions in more creative ways than just watching TV news channels all day (I’m certainly someone who has done that in the past). 

Aside from this, there have been so many opportunities created in virtual online spaces and the radio industry has stepped up to the mark for both, audiences and future potential presenters. From online festivals like the virtual Radio 1’s Big Weekend and Absolute Radio hosting a pop-up station to mark the 75th anniversary of VE day to online lectures being hosted by the SRA, there have been so many things to contribute to and get involved with that it has made the past few months fly! I do hope that, maybe next year, these online features continue – because they have been so fulfilling. 

Remember when people said that Boeing 747 shrank the earth and led to us forging deep connections with people thousands of miles away? At the end of July, Radio 1 set the ‘Up Yours Corona’ challenge – as someone fully invested in radio, I have loved following the journey and the excitement of connecting to not only other fellow UK citizens, but fellow residents of the world! Thus, what features such as this throughout the radio industry have done is not only keep us informed creatively, but made us feel that we are all in this situation together, we are not alone, and that there is power and strength in uniting over something as distressing and life-changing as Coronavirus. To this end, I am proud to have been involved with student radio over this challenging period – it has shown just how united we can be, and this can only be a positive thing. 

 

Thank you, everyone, who took part!
